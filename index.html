Project Goals¶

The COVID-19 pandemic caused widespread disruptions to daily life, including significant changes to how students experienced education. This project aims to examine the impact of COVID-19 on public school education in New Orleans, Louisiana. Specifically, it analyzes changes in school performance scores (SPS) and student progress indices across multiple schools, comparing trends from the pre-pandemic period (2016–2019) to the post-pandemic years (2020–2025). In addition to this, the project also explores variation across schools to determine whether recovery has been uniform or uneven. By evaluating both overall performance and measures of student growth, this analysis seeks to identify the extent of academic disruption and assess patterns of recovery across schools over time.

Research Questions:

  1. Is student progress index a significant predictor of school performance score (SPS)?
  2. Did COVID-19 cause a statistically significant decline in average school performance scores?

Dataset 1: Pre-COVID Data¶

The first dataset used in this analysis consists of school performance score (SPS) data obtained from the Louisiana Department of Education. We collected SPS datasets for the years 2016–2019, representing the pre-COVID period. Each dataset contains school-level performance metrics, including overall performance scores and related indicators used to evaluate academic achievement. To prepare the data for analysis, the individual yearly datasets were cleaned and standardized to ensure consistent column names and formats across years. These datasets were then combined into a single dataframe covering the full 2016–2019 period. This consolidated dataset allows for efficient comparison of school performance trends prior to the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic.

Data Cleaning (ETL)¶

To prepare the datasets for analysis, we performed several cleaning and transformation steps to ensure consistency and usability across years:

  • Standardized all column names to lowercase with underscores
  • Melted wide-format yearly data into a tidy long format (one row per school per year)
  • Extracted the numeric year from column names and constructed a readable school_year label
  • Removed duplicate rows created by overlapping year columns across datasets
  • Retained only the three columns needed for analysis: school_name, school_year, and school_performance_score_sps

# Helper function to standardize column names across all yearly datasets
def clean_columns(df):
    df.columns = (
        df.columns
        .str.lower()
        .str.strip()
        .str.replace("\n", "_")
        .str.replace(" ", "_")
        .str.replace(r"[()]", "", regex=True)
    )
    return df

Interesting Statistics¶

Key statistics from the post-COVID dataset include:

  • There are 75 unique schools included in this dataset.
  • The average performance score has increased since the 2020–2021 school year, rising from approximately 64.53 to 74.67.
  • Benjamin Franklin High School had the highest performance score of 133.9, occurring in the 2024–2025 school year.
  • Thurgood Marshall had the lowest performance score of 40.9, occurring in the 2020–2021 school year.

These statistics inform us of when the highest and lowest performance scores occurred, as well as the overall average across years. This gives us an initial indication of performance score recovery trends since the pandemic.

Model Questions¶

The exploratory data analysis above supports the following two modeling approaches:

Model Question 1: Predicting School Performance from Student Progress¶

Question: Is student progress index a significant predictor of school performance score (SPS) in post-COVID New Orleans schools?

  • Dependent Variable: school_performance_score_sps
  • Independent Variable: progress_index
  • Methodology: Simple linear regression. We will fit a least-squares regression line, report the coefficient and R² value, and evaluate statistical significance via the p-value of the slope.
  • Evaluation: R² (proportion of variance explained), RMSE (average prediction error), and a residual plot to check for violations of linearity.
  • EDA Support: Graph 6 shows a clear positive relationship between progress_index and SPS, suggesting that a linear model is a reasonable starting point.

Model Question 2: Impact of COVID-19 on School Performance¶

Question: Did COVID-19 cause a statistically significant decline in average school performance scores in New Orleans public schools?

  • Dependent Variable: school_performance_score_sps
  • Independent Variable: period — a binary variable coded as 0 for Pre-COVID (2015–2019) and 1 for Post-COVID (2020–2025)
  • Methodology: Two-sample independent t-test (H₀: mean SPS pre-COVID = mean SPS post-COVID; H₁: mean SPS pre-COVID > mean SPS post-COVID). We will report the t-statistic, p-value, and 95% confidence interval for the difference in means.
  • Evaluation: Statistical significance at α = 0.05, and a visual comparison using overlapping histograms or a box plot of pre- vs. post-COVID SPS.
  • EDA Support: Graph 2 shows a visible drop in average SPS at the COVID start year, which motivates testing whether this difference is statistically significant rather than due to chance variation.

Model Justification¶

Both models are directly informed by the EDA conducted above. The consistent upward trend in average SPS post-COVID (Graph 3), the visible drop at the COVID onset (Graph 2), and the positive progress-performance relationship (Graph 6) together provide strong justification for pursuing these two quantitative questions in subsequent milestones.

Conclusions¶

This project has established a solid analytical foundation for studying the impact of COVID-19 on New Orleans public school performance. Using two datasets — Louisiana Department of Education SPS data (2015–2019) and NOLA Public Schools academic metrics (2020–2025) — we have:

  • Cleaned and combined both datasets into a tidy, analysis-ready format
  • Confirmed that school performance scores dipped at the onset of COVID-19 and have been recovering since
  • Identified a strong positive relationship between student progress index and overall school performance
  • Observed variation in the rate of recovery across school years

In the next milestone, we will build the two models outlined above: a linear regression predicting SPS from progress index, and a t-test comparing pre- and post-COVID performance.
